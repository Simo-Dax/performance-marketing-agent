# VOC Injection Map

The hardcoded mapping of which VOC content goes in which page slot. This is the competitive moat of this skill. Other landing page generators treat VOC as freeform input or skip it entirely. This skill hardcodes the slot-to-content-type mapping because each slot has a different content type that converts.

## Core principle

VOC research captures customer language verbatim. The page should sound like the customer speaking, not like a marketer guessing. The exact map below tells you which sections benefit from raw quotes and which need polished outcomes.

## The map

| Page slot | VOC content type | Editorial rule | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| H1 | **DO NOT** put raw VOC here | H1 must echo ad headline for message match (3+ word phrase) | (from ad) "Lose 20 lbs without giving up coffee" |
| H1 subhead | One customer's stated outcome, lightly polished | One sentence, present tense, no quote marks | "Finally stopped waking up tired and bloated." |
| Above fold trust line | Aggregate proof point from VOC or Brand DNA | Numbers only if real | "4,200 customers since 2022. 4.8 star average across 1,847 reviews." |
| Problem agitation opener | Verbatim pain quote with attribution | Use quote marks. Cite name + 1 detail | "I tried 4 different programs and gained the weight back every time." Sarah, 38, Portland |
| Mechanism or How It Works | Customer's own description of using the product | Lightly polished, never invented | "Take 2 capsules with breakfast. That's it." |
| Benefit bullets | Outcomes phrased in customer language | Concrete, never vague | "Sharper focus by 10am" not "Better focus" |
| Comparison table | Customer's own framing of alternatives | "vs the brand they tried before" structure | Pulled from VOC "what I used to use" mentions |
| Testimonials block | Whole verbatim quotes with photos | Include name, role or age, location if available. Mix one short, one medium, one long | "..." Sarah, 38, Portland (with photo) |
| Founder or Origin story | Brand DNA origin block if it exists | If thin, use VOC pattern "the founder gets it" quotes | "Mike actually responded to my email at 11pm" |
| FAQ questions | **VERBATIM** customer questions from VOC | Use the customer's exact phrasing, not a rewrite | "Can I cancel anytime?" not "What is your cancellation policy?" |
| FAQ answers | Brand response in Brand DNA voice | Honest, specific, no marketing speak | "Yes. Cancel from your account dashboard. Refund processes in 3 business days." |
| Final CTA microcopy | Reassurance line lifted from VOC or Brand DNA | Direct, human, addresses the last objection | "If it does not work, I will refund you personally. Mike, founder." |

## What goes in the H1

The H1 is the most important slot on the page and the only slot where VOC does NOT belong directly. Why, the H1 must match the ad headline for conversion. Putting a different message in the H1, even if it is great VOC, breaks message match and tanks conversion.

**Rule:** The H1 must contain at least one 3+ word phrase from the verbatim ad headline.

Examples:

| Ad headline | Valid H1 | Invalid H1 |
|---|---|---|
| "Sleep deeper in 3 nights or your money back" | "Sleep deeper in 3 nights. Or get every penny back." | "Finally rest your tired mind" |
| "The notebook that thinks with you" | "The notebook that thinks with you, on every page." | "AI for your daily notes" |
| "Lose 20 lbs without giving up coffee" | "Lose 20 lbs without giving up your morning coffee." | "Drop weight the smart way" |

The valid H1 carries the phrase from the ad forward. The invalid H1 is technically fine copy but breaks message match.

## What goes in the subhead

The subhead is the highest leverage VOC slot. This is where a real customer's stated outcome lands. Polish the grammar minimally. Keep the voice.

**Rule:** Pick the strongest single outcome from VOC. Make it one sentence. Present tense. No quote marks (the subhead is the brand speaking, but in customer voice).

Examples:

| Raw VOC quote | Polished subhead |
|---|---|
| "i used to wake up SO tired no matter how much i slept but now im up at 6 wide awake" | "Wake up at 6 wide awake, even on the nights you sleep less." |
| "took me about a week to notice but my focus is so much better, i used to lose track of what i was reading by paragraph 3" | "Sharper focus by week two. No more losing track of what you were reading." |
| "best part is i dont have to give up bread or wine which other diets always made me do" | "Lose the weight without giving up bread or wine." |

The pattern, the customer said the thing, you compressed it, you kept the voice.

## What goes in the problem agitation

This is VOC slot two. The most leverage comes from a single verbatim pain quote with attribution. Use quote marks. Cite name + one detail (age, role, city, occupation).

**Rule:** Pick the quote that captures the moment of frustration most viscerally. Do not paraphrase. Quote marks are mandatory.

Pattern:

```
"I tried 4 different programs and gained the weight back every time."
Sarah, 38, Portland
```

Not:

```
Many customers report frustration with traditional weight loss approaches.
```

## What goes in the FAQ

FAQ is VOC slot three in leverage. This is where the page becomes an objection handling machine. The questions come VERBATIM from VOC. Do not invent them. Do not professionalize the phrasing.

If VOC contains:

- "wait, does this have caffeine?"
- "is it safe for pregnant women?"
- "how long until i see results?"

The FAQ questions are:

- Does it have caffeine?
- Is it safe for pregnant women?
- How long until I see results?

Not:

- What are the active ingredients?
- Who should consult a physician before using this product?
- What is the expected timeline to observe results?

The first set sounds like the customer. The second set sounds like a lawyer. The first set converts.

## What goes in the testimonials

Whole quotes. Include the customer's real name, role or age, location, and (if available) a real photo. Mix lengths:

- One short punchy testimonial, 1 to 2 sentences, hits one specific benefit.
- One medium testimonial, 3 to 4 sentences, tells a mini story.
- One longer testimonial, 5 to 7 sentences, walks through the before, during, after.

Do not invent testimonials. Do not invent attribution. If VOC has fewer than 3 usable testimonials, use what is real and adjust the section (a logo strip + 1 testimonial, or a star rating block instead of 3 cards).

## What goes in the final CTA microcopy

The one or two lines under the final CTA button. This is reassurance, not selling. Pull from VOC reasons-to-trust or from Brand DNA voice.

Patterns that work:

- A founder personal guarantee, "If it does not work, I will refund you personally. Mike, founder."
- A specific operational fact, "Ships from Copenhagen. Tracked delivery. 30 day returns."
- A customer count cite, "4,200 customers since 2022."
- A risk reversal, "Try it for 60 days. If you don't feel it, full refund. No questions."

Avoid:

- "Join thousands of happy customers" (vague)
- "Risk free purchase" (no specifics)
- "Limited time offer" (cold traffic does not believe it)

## Hard rules

1. Every word in every section comes from VOC, Brand DNA, the ad creative, or operational fact. Nothing invented.
2. Verbatim quotes use quote marks. Polished outcomes do not.
3. Attribution required for every testimonial and pain quote.
4. FAQ questions are VERBATIM customer phrasing. Never rewritten.
5. If VOC is thin in a slot, use less content rather than invent.
6. Numbers (customer counts, review counts, star ratings) come from Brand DNA or VOC, never from imagination.
