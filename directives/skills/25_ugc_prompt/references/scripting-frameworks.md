# Ad Scripting Frameworks 2026

A reference library of 12 short-form video ad scripting frameworks, plus a set of static-ad prompt templates.

**How to read this doc**
- Each framework has: a one-line **purpose**, an **acronym breakdown** (each beat → its goal → a worked example), and an **example briefing** laid out as a beat-by-beat table.
- Briefing tables use these columns: **Beat** = the framework step · **Script** = spoken voiceover/UGC line · **On-Screen Text** = caption shown on screen · **B-Roll** = footage/shot direction.
- All original brand names, product names, founder names, source codes, and production notes are retained. The only thing removed is the original publisher's internal branding (the "Epic Ads Lab / EAL" labels and swipe-file links).

## 1. Bandwagon Effect — C.R.O.W.D.

*Source ad: 250310_BIOGHK (Soothe) · variants V1, V2*

**Purpose:** Use social momentum — what experts, authorities, or crowds are already doing — to pull the viewer toward your solution.

### Framework breakdown

- **C — Call out the movement:** Create curiosity by calling out what relevant groups, experts, or authority figures are doing, trying, or recommending that relates to your solution.
  - *Example:* If you sell ovens that use light technology and someone famous switched to that kind of oven, call it out in your hook.
- **R — Reject the old way:** Educate the viewer on why the common solution isn't as effective as they think. This sets up your solution as superior.
  - *Example (plant-based leather):* "Animal leather cracks within 2 years while poisoning rivers with chromium — but brands still charge $500 for it."
- **O — Onboard the new way:** Introduce your product/service as the superior solution.
  - *Example (sleep tech):* "DeepPulse isn't an app — it's a pillow insert that uses medical-grade EEG to actually track sleep stages."
- **W — Wave the proof:** Build trust with social proof, studies, and case studies.
  - *Example (Lightwave Oven):* "12 Michelin chefs adopted it last quarter — see @ChefRevolt's perfect salmon cook in 47 seconds."
- **D — Direct the viewer:** Get viewers to check out your product/service.
  - *Example (sleep tech):* "Stop guessing your sleep → grab DeepPulse now and wake up refreshed (60-day proof guarantee)."
  - *Bonus tip:* Reinforce the main benefit of your solution inside the CTA.

## 2. Industry Contrarian — D.I.S.R.U.P.T.

*Source brand: Soothe (GHK-Cu)*

**Purpose:** Position your product as the truth the industry has been hiding — expose what's broken, then reveal your solution.

### Framework breakdown

- **D — Declassify the secret:** Expose suppressed industry knowledge or debunk a myth.
  - *Example (skincare):* Reveal how clinical studies on copper peptides (GHK-Cu) were underfunded for 30 years despite proven anti-aging effects, because skincare brands prioritized cheaper synthetic ingredients.
- **I — Industry failure:** Explain why mainstream solutions fail.
  - *Example (food tech):* Show how "healthy" cereal brands use processed starches that spike blood sugar more than table sugar, despite "whole grain" marketing.
- **S — Solution:** Introduce your solution.
  - *Example (sleep tech):* Position non-EMF sleep trackers as biologically safer than smartphones in bed, using infrared sensors instead of radiation-emitting Bluetooth.
- **R — Reinforce with evidence:** Validate with high-trust assets — studies, case studies, scientific discoveries, real-life stories.
  - *Example (fitness):* Cite University of Tampa research showing bodyweight training builds muscle 40% faster than machines for beginners.
- **U — Upstage the competition:** Show what makes your solution unique.
  - *Example (SaaS):* No-code tools build funnels in 1 hour vs. $15,000 agency fees and 3-month timelines.
- **P — Provide proof:** Show measurable real-world adoption (social proof).
  - *Example (renewable energy):* 12,000+ homes eliminated power bills using compact vertical wind turbines where solar wasn't viable.
- **T — Trigger the movement:** Call to action.
  - *Example (all industries):* Offer 30-day free returns if the solution doesn't outperform the status quo.

## 3. Listicle — C.U.R.E.

*Source brand: Vitauthority (Multi Collagen Burn Powder)*

**Purpose:** A numbered "mistakes you're making" rundown that exposes flawed solutions, then positions yours as the fix.

### Framework breakdown

- **Curiosity (list hook):** Spark intrigue, then give the reason for the number.
  - *Example (sleep aid):* ❌ "5 Hidden Mistakes Making Your Sleep Worse (Not Better)" → ✅ "If you got 8h of sleep but still wake up tired, here are 5 mistakes making your sleep worse."
- **Uncover failures (current-solution flaws):** Expose why standard approaches fail.
  - *Example (sleep aid):* 1) Melatonin supplements that disrupt natural hormone balance long-term; 2) White noise machines that drown out vital environmental alerts; 3) Sleep trackers causing anxiety through inaccurate REM data.
- **Remedy (product benefits):** Introduce your solution as the answer.
  - *Example (sleep aid):* "NeuroRestore uses chronobiotic amino acids to help you get better sleep."
- **Evidence (trust building):** Add credibility through social proof.
  - *Example (sleep aid):* "87% of users achieved deeper sleep in 2 weeks in a validated trial."
- **Engage (CTA):** Drive action with risk reversal.
  - *Example (sleep aid):* "Try risk-free for 30 nights — track your sleep-quality improvement or get a full refund."

## 4. Founder — F.O.U.N.D.E.R.

*Source ad: 250217_FOUNDERWC (Community Wolf) · variant V1*

**Purpose:** Tell the founder's origin story so the brand becomes a relatable human on a mission the buyer wants to join.

### Framework breakdown

- **Feature the founder:** Introduce the founder(s) as a relatable, empathetic human behind a mission that benefits the ideal buyer.
  - *Example (EduTech):* "Meet Sarah — a teacher who saw her students struggling with math."
- **Opposing force:** Present the enemy/problem your ideal buyer faces.
  - *Example (climate tech):* "1.2 billion people face water scarcity."
- **Unlocking the idea:** Reveal the breakthrough moment; frame your solution as an intuitive pivot.
  - *Example (food waste):* "What if we could turn grocery overstock into meals for hungry families?"
- **Numbers:** Present 3–4 tangible outcomes (numbers, timelines, revenue).
  - *Example (renewable energy):* "12 weeks → 200 homes off-grid, 3,000 tons of CO₂ saved, 50% lower utility bills."
- **Destiny:** Position current wins as just the beginning of a mission that aligns with your buyer's desires and values.
  - *Example (HealthTech):* "India, Nigeria, Philippines — 500M people need this diabetes-reversal protocol."
- **Engage the movement:** A CTA that frames supporters as co-revolutionaries — use "join" language and position the action as an identity shift ("become part of…").
  - *Example (EdTech):* "Become an education revolutionary — sponsor a classroom now."

## 5. How You Can X With/Without Y — S.I.M.P.L.E.

*Source ad: 241219_DADBB (Three Beacon)*

**Purpose:** Promise the outcome they want without the effort they dread — the "one-thing fix" for a busy identity.

### Framework breakdown

- **S — State the escape:** Address identity + pain in one line.
  - *Example (meal prep):* "How to eat healthy without Sunday cooking marathons."
- **I — Identify pain:** Mirror the audience's frustrations with specific scenarios that agitate the problem.
  - *Example (fitness):* "Your garage gym collects dust because after work you're too drained for hour-long workouts."
- **M — Minimal solution:** Introduce your product as the "one-thing fix" — position it as the only thing they need, in simple language.
  - *Example (productivity):* "Our app consolidates 7 tools into one 5-minute daily habit."
- **P — Prove:** Connect features to benefits with "because → so" logic.
  - *Example (energy drink):* Guayusa → steady focus without jitters; electrolytes → hydration without bloat; B vitamins → energy without crash.
- **L — Lifestyle win:** Contrast past effort with the desired outcome that now takes minimal effort.
  - *Example (finance):* "No spreadsheets — just tap and watch savings grow while you parent."
- **E — Escape CTA:** Urge action with identity-tailored minimalism.
  - *Example (sleep):* "If you're an entrepreneur who can't sleep but won't meditate → try SleepCaps tonight."

## 6. Organic — P.U.R.E.

*Source ad: 250313_WORLDGTD (Alleyoop) · variants V1–V5*

**Purpose:** Make the ad feel like a real, unpolished discovery — raw problem, messy real life, genuine proof, effortless payoff.

### Framework breakdown

- **P — Problem uncovered:** Hook with raw, relatable frustration; use blunt language exposing the pain point.
  - *Example (deodorant):* "My pits smelled like a gym bag by noon — until this."
- **U — Unfiltered chaos:** Show how the product's benefits show up in everyday life; 2–3 real scenarios.
  - *Example (footwear):* 1) My feet stayed dry walking in the rain for an hour. 2) After a muddy dog walk, my feet were the driest part of me.
- **R — Real evidence:** Prove performance through organic discovery — genuine "aha!" moments in natural environments.
  - *Example (backpack):* "Got home, dumped the bag: laptop dry despite the monsoon outside."
- **E — Effortless freedom:** Reveal the anti-hassle payoff; contrast the easy solution against previous struggles.
  - *Example (meal kit):* "20 min → done. No dishes, no grocery runs."

## 7. PAS — Pain · Agitate · Solution

*Source ad: 250401_ANXMLB (Three Beacon Marketing) · variants V1–V5*

**Purpose:** Name a raw pain, twist the knife emotionally, then deliver the product as the precise relief.

### Framework breakdown

- **Problem (relatable struggle):** Address a core pain point.
  - *Example:* "Anxiety attacks leave you gasping, unable to breathe deeply despite advice."
  - *Key phrases:* "can't catch your breath" feeling · "lungs felt too weak" · "chest-tightening panic."
  - *Psychological hooks:* emotional vulnerability (the "drowning" metaphor); failed solutions ("just breathe" advice backfires).
- **Agitate (intensify the pain):** Show how the pain affects them on a deeper emotional level.
  1. **Physical suffering:** "Gasping for air… feeling like drowning" → triggers body memory of panic.
  2. **Hopelessness:** "Deep breathing alone never helped… breathing always felt weak" → makes the viewer feel trapped.
  3. **Isolation:** implies others don't understand the severity.
  - *Triggers:* loss aversion ("this will always be your reality"); social-proof gap ("others cope — why can't you?").
- **Solution (your product):** Present your product as the solution.
  1. **Unique mechanism:** "Unlike basic exercises → dual resistance trains inhale/exhale muscles" → "why this works when nothing else did."
  2. **Relief:** "Within 10 breaths: heart slowed, mind cleared" → speed = credibility.
  3. **Social backing:** "1000s of people love it" → FOMO + reduced skepticism.
  - *Payoff:* certainty anchor ("this WILL work for you"); identity shift ("from victim to prepared warrior").

| Hook 5 | Here's a science-backed way to force your body to take deep breaths when you need to the most | Science-backed way to get through anxiety spikes |

## 8. Ugly Ads — U.G.L.Y.

*Source ad: 250103_PAPER (Switch Research) · variants V1–V5*

**Purpose:** Deliberately unpolished, cheap-looking, rule-breaking creative that stops the scroll because it *doesn't* look like an ad.

> **U.G.L.Y. = Unpolished • Gritty • Lean • Yelling-for-action**

Apply these 3 questions to ANY structure (PAS, AIDA, etc.):

### 1. "How can I make this SIMPLER?"
Strip complexity until it hurts.
- **Ask:** "Can I say this in 5 words or less?" · "What 70% of words can I delete?" · "Would a 10-year-old understand this?"
- **Tactics:** Replace sentences with bullet-point text cards.
  - *Before:* "Our journal helps you process emotions through CBT techniques." → *After:* "ANGER JOURNAL → THERAPIST VIDS + CBT PROMPTS → CALM"
  - *Example (fintech):* "Automatically invest spare change from purchases." → "TURN $0.83 COFFEE CHANGE → STOCKS. SET & FORGET."

### 2. "How can I show this CHEAPLY?"
Embrace "poor" production values.
- **Ask:** "What can I film with a $0 budget?" · "How would a bored intern present this?" · "Which free editing app effect looks tacky but eye-catching?"
- **Tactics:** Shoot vertically on a phone with natural lighting (shadows welcome); use MS-Paint-style graphics (crude arrows, neon text).
  - *Example (meal replacement):* High-res chef stirring powder in a blender → selfie video of an exhausted mom dumping powder into a water bottle while a toddler screams. Text overlay: "30 SECONDS → LUNCH DONE."

### 3. "What's the OPPOSITE of competitors?"
Sabotage polished ad tropes.
- **Ask:** "What would make my creative director cry?" · "Which industry 'rule' can I violently break?" · "What truth is everyone avoiding?"
- **Tactics:** Swap beauty shots for unflattering reality (flawless model applying serum → close-up of flaky red skin + cracked product tube); use negative emotions (anger, envy, shame) instead of joy.
  - *Example (job platform):* "Find your dream career!" → "HATE YOUR BOSS? CLICK HERE TO STEAL HIS JOB."

## 9. Founder Objections — P.R.O.V.E.

*Source ad: 250217_IMPOSSWC (Community Wolf) · variant V1*

**Purpose:** A founder-led, objection-crushing story — name the doubt everyone had, then prove them wrong with results.

### Framework breakdown

- **P — Problem (stark reality hook):** Lead with an undeniable, painful truth your market faces.
  - *Example (cybersecurity):* "Last year, hackers stole $20B from small businesses because firewalls can't stop insider threats."
- **R — Reframe (founder's credibility):** Contrast the critics with your identity.
  - *Example (food-waste tech):* "But we're farmers — we know 40% of food rots in transit while families starve."
- **O — Objection (name the doubt):** Voice the specific skepticism you overcame.

- **V — Victory (proof):** Crush the doubt with tangible results.

- **E — Expand (bigger vision):** Pivot to the bigger ambition that appeals to your buyer.

## 11. Triple G — Goal · Gap · Gains

**Purpose:** Anchor to the buyer's goal, expose the missing piece that's blocked them, then position your product as the bridge.

### Framework breakdown

- **G — Goal (tap into their aspiration):** Identify the specific outcome/transformation they want. Use year-specific language for urgency.
- **G — Gap (reveal what's missing):** Expose why previous attempts failed; position the missing tool/knowledge/approach they didn't have.
- **G — Gains (show the transformation):** Present your product as the bridge that closes the gap; stack proof that it works.

**Output required:**
1. Complete ad copy following the Triple G framework
2. 5 hook variations using the Goal–Gap–Gains structure
3. Proof-stack positioning
4. CTA with urgency

### Example output
- **Goal:** "Make hydration a habit in 2025"
- **Gap:** "Current drinks are loaded with sugar and junk"
- **Gains:** "Zero-sugar, zero-junk electrolyte solution"

**5 hook variations:**
1. *Goal-question hook:* "[Goal outcome]? Let's change that for 2025"
2. *Gap-reveal hook:* "Still failing at [goal] because you're missing this"
3. *Tool-bridge hook:* "The BEST tool to achieve [goal outcome]"
4. *Habit-gap hook:* "Make [goal] a habit with [missing piece]"
5. *Year-goal hook:* "Your 2025 [goal] starts with fixing this gap"

## 12. The TEASE Framework (Curiosity Loop)

*Source: Valentine's campaign — The Essence Vault*

**Purpose:** Open with a claim that can't be true yet, then keep the viewer watching until the loop resolves.

### Framework breakdown

- **T — Tease:** Create curiosity with an impossible/surprising claim (cognitive dissonance).
- **E — Engage:** Build the story context and journey.
- **A — Advantage:** Show the benefits and value.
- **S — Satisfy:** Resolve the curiosity loop.
- **E — Encourage:** Call to action.

### Curiosity-loop analysis

| Component | Script content | Purpose |
|-----------|----------------|---------|
| T — Tease (loop start) | "I got my boyfriend a Valentine's gift for free." | Immediate curiosity — how is a gift "free"? A hook that demands explanation. |
| E — Engage (story development) | "So I went on that fishing date with my boyfriend and you know, I needed that extra oomph. So of course I used my favorite perfume from The Essence Vault, F number 82." | Sets the scene and introduces the "secret weapon" without revealing its connection to the opening claim. |
| A — Advantage (benefits shown) | "I swear by this stuff. It's just so affordable and long-lasting. Anyways, I am not joking — my boyfriend was all over me. It was so funny. He kept asking me how much money I spent on this perfume, and when I told him it was from the Essence Vault, he did not believe me." | Shows multiple benefits: affordability, effectiveness, desirability, premium perception at a low cost. |
| S — Satisfy (payoff / resolution) | "And then he started saying, because I'd saved so much money buying from the Essence Vault, I could pay for this date. Which I replied no." | Reveals the loop — the "free gift" was the date itself: she saved so much on affordable perfume that the boyfriend joked she could pay, making his gift to her effectively "free." |
| E — Encourage (CTA) | "And you can now get 30% off plus a free gift worth up to £15 this Valentine's Day. Check it out." | Immediate action with urgency and an additional incentive. |

### Complete framework application
- **Tease:** an impossible/surprising claim that creates cognitive dissonance
- **Engage:** a story that builds toward resolution while introducing the product
- **Advantage:** stacking benefits and social proof within the narrative
- **Satisfy:** a clever resolution that makes the initial claim logical
- **Encourage:** an immediate CTA with urgency and a bonus

### Hook variations using TEASE
1. "I got my boyfriend a Valentine's gift for free"
2. "My boyfriend paid for our entire Valentine's date (and he doesn't know it)"
3. "This €12 perfume saved me €200 on Valentine's Day"
4. "How I turned a £10 purchase into a free romantic dinner"
5. "The Valentine's hack that made my boyfriend pay for everything"

> **Why it works:** it creates a logical puzzle that can only be solved by watching the entire ad, ensuring high retention and engagement.

## 13. Static Ad Prompt Templates

Copy-paste prompts for generating static (image) ads.

**Guide for all prompts:**
- Use your standard direct-response copywriting GPT / custom messaging prompt for the copy.
- Use an image-generation model (e.g. ChatGPT / GPT-4o image generation) for the visuals when needed.

### 1. Offer Static
*Inputs: audience research document + product/service information.*

**Copywriting prompt:**
```
You are an expert direct response copywriter. I will give you two inputs: an
Offer Description and an Audience Research Document, and I need you to produce:

1. Ten bold, benefit-led headlines tailored to this offer and audience
2. Ten action-focused CTAs that drive that audience to convert

Always draw on the audience's pain points, desires, language and objections as
described.

• OFFER: [insert your full offer description here]
```

**Image prompt** (paste one headline + CTA and a static example/template):
```
Generate a square social media image that shows this headline and CTA:
Headline: [paste headline]
CTA: [paste CTA]
Use the static example as a guideline for how it should look.
*Optional:* Include the image I attached of [product / result / etc.]
```

*Inputs: a real customer review + product/service information.*

**Prompt:**
```
Generate a social-media static ad featuring:

- Our logo in the top left and a small button in the bottom right reading
  [INSERT CTA]
```

### 3. PR Callout Static
*Inputs: audience research document + product/service information.*

**Copywriting prompt:**
```
You are an expert direct response copywriter. I will give you two inputs:
Product/Service information and an Audience Research Document, and I need you to
output two numbered lists:

1. Ten punchy, news-style headline variations that read like editorial callouts
   (e.g. "Why Everyone's Talking About…" or "The Innovation Changing…").
2. Ten concise sub-headline variations that expand on the news hook and hint at
   what makes this product innovative or why people are switching.

Always lean into the "As seen in…" or "Breaking:" feel, position the offer as a
trend or authority story, and speak the language of the audience.
```

**Image prompt** (choose a headline + sub-headline, add a template/example and a product-in-action image):
```
Create a clean, editorial-style static ad image featuring:
- A bold, top-center headline: "[INSERT CHOSEN HEADLINE HERE]" in serif or
  high-contrast sans-serif.
- Directly beneath, a smaller sub-headline: "[INSERT CHOSEN SUB-HEADLINE HERE]"
- A crisp shot or mock-up of [PRODUCT/SERVICE] off to one side.
- Use the template / example as reference so this is laid out to mirror an
  article.
```
