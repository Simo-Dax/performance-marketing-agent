# Winning Ad Analysis Framework ,  What Made This One Win

When you receive the user's winning ad image, analyze every element below. Be precise and specific. The goal of this analysis is different from a competitor rebuild. Here you are extracting the conversion DNA so you can preserve it in variations while deliberately diverging in scene, hook, and copy.

---

## 1. STRUCTURAL SKELETON (preserve across variations)

This is the layout pattern that made the ad work. It must be recognizably preserved across every variation, even as the scene and copy change.

- **Format and dimensions:** Square (1:1), portrait (4:5 or 9:16), or landscape (1.91:1)?
- **Visual zones:** How is the image divided? Describe the regions. Example: "top 60% is product hero on lifestyle background, bottom 40% is dark text block with white headline"
- **Text hierarchy:** Where does each text element sit and how is it weighted? (largest to smallest)
- **Product position:** Centered, left-anchored, right-anchored, hero, environmental, hand-held, etc.
- **Negative space pattern:** Where is the empty space and what role does it play?
- **Overall density:** Minimal/clean or dense/busy?

Record this as the structural skeleton. Every variation must preserve this skeleton.

---

## 2. TEXT INVENTORY

For every piece of text in the ad, record the word count and role. Word counts feed the prompt-builder so each variation can match the visual balance.

**HEADLINE (largest/most prominent text):**
- Exact text (if legible) or approximate content
- Word count: [X words]
- Character count including spaces: [approximately X characters]
- Font weight: bold / semi-bold / regular
- Font size relative to image: large / medium / small
- Color: [hex if determinable, or description]
- Position: [where on the image]
- All caps / title case / sentence case

**SUBHEADLINE or SECONDARY TEXT (if present):**
- Exact text or approximate content
- Word count: [X words]
- Character count including spaces: [approximately X characters]
- Font weight, size, color, position

**BODY COPY (if present):**
- Exact text or approximate content
- Word count: [X words]
- Line count: [X lines]
- Position and size relative to headline

**CTA / BUTTON TEXT (if present):**
- Exact text or close approximation
- Word count: [X words]
- Style: button / plain text / badge
- Position

**BADGE, LABEL, or CALLOUT (if present):**
- Exact or approximate text
- Word count: [X words]
- Style and position

**OVERLAY TEXT or GRAPHIC TEXT:**
- Any text integrated into the visual scene
- Word count for each piece

---

## 3. THE CONVERSION MECHANIC (this is the most important section)

The conversion mechanic is the structural reason this ad works. It is what every variation must preserve even as the angle and scene change. Identify it precisely.

Pick the dominant mechanic from this list (or describe a hybrid):

- **Headline-led:** The headline is the ad. The visual is clean support. Mechanic: copy carries the conversion weight.
- **Claim/result-led:** A specific, falsifiable result or stat is the focal point. Mechanic: proof of outcome.
- **Testimonial-led:** A customer quote or review is the focal point. Mechanic: social proof grounded in a real voice.
- **Demo-led:** The product is shown in use or its mechanism is visible. Mechanic: showing not telling.
- **Comparison-led:** Two states or two options shown side by side. Mechanic: contrast triggers preference.
- **Offer-led:** Discount, deal, or promotion is the focal point. Mechanic: urgency plus value.
- **Lifestyle-led:** The ad sells the identity, environment, or moment more than the product. Mechanic: aspiration.
- **Pattern-interrupt-led:** Something visually unexpected or tonally contrarian. Mechanic: scroll-stopping novelty.
- **List-led:** Bullets, callouts, or feature points dominate. Mechanic: fast-scan benefit absorption.
- **UGC-led:** The ad mimics user-generated content (phone-shot, casual, raw). Mechanic: native feel disarms ad resistance.

Then write 1 to 2 sentences explaining specifically how this mechanic operates in this ad. Example: "Testimonial-led with a five-star anchor. The white quote text on the lifestyle background does the heavy lifting. The product is intentionally slightly out of focus so the viewer reads the quote first."

---

## 4. CURRENT HOOK ANGLE (variations will deliberately differ)

What is the hook angle of this winning ad right now? Pick the closest match:

- **Curiosity gap:** Creates an information gap that demands resolution
- **Bold claim:** Specific, falsifiable result or statistic
- **Pattern interrupt:** Shows or says something unexpected
- **Relatability:** Mirrors the customer's lived experience precisely
- **Social proof:** Adoption numbers, testimonials, peer behavior
- **Fear/loss:** What they stand to lose by not acting
- **Aspiration:** Paints the desired identity or outcome
- **Problem-agitation:** Names and amplifies the pain
- **Direct offer:** Discount, free trial, or limited-time deal

Note this hook so the variation engine can deliberately pick DIFFERENT ones across the variation set.

---

## 5. CURRENT AWARENESS LEVEL (variations will spread across multiple)

Which Schwartz stage does the winning ad target?

- **Unaware:** No mention of problem or product, identity or lifestyle led
- **Problem-Aware:** Names the pain, no solution yet
- **Solution-Aware:** Category-level, no brand name
- **Product-Aware:** Brand or product specific
- **Most-Aware:** Direct offer, price, urgency

Note this so variations can target other stages and unlock different audience segments.

---

## 6. CURRENT VISUAL SCENE AND COLOR WORLD (variations will diverge from this)

Describe the existing scene precisely so variations can pick deliberately different ones:

- **Setting/environment:** Studio, kitchen, bathroom, outdoor, gym, bedroom, workspace, etc.
- **Lighting:** Soft natural, harsh studio, golden hour, dim moody, bright clinical, etc.
- **Background style:** Solid color, gradient, lifestyle scene, textured, etc.
- **Color palette:** List the 2 to 4 dominant colors and the mood (warm/cool, bold/muted, dark/light)
- **Product context:** Hero on clean surface, in-use, hand-held, environmental, flatlay, etc.
- **Human element:** Person present? Face visible? Body language? Or no human at all?
- **Props or context objects:** What else is in the frame?
- **Image style:** Photographic, illustrated, graphic design, collage, screenshot, mockup
- **Social proof visual elements:** Stars, logos, review snippets, certification badges?

This is the baseline scene. Every variation must use a different scene AND a different color world.

---

## 7. WHY THIS AD IS WINNING

Write 3 to 5 bullet points on why this ad is likely performing. Consider:
- The specific hook and why it stops the scroll
- The clarity and simplicity of the promise
- The visual contrast or focal point that draws the eye
- The awareness level match to the audience
- Any trust signals present
- The format efficiency

This is your strategic brief. Every variation must honor these reasons even while diverging visually.

---

## 8. PRESERVE/CHANGE LIST

Write a clear list of what stays vs what changes across variations.

**STAYS the same across all variations:**
- The structural skeleton (layout zones, text hierarchy, product position pattern)
- The brand identity (colors as defined in Brand DNA, logo placement)
- The product (rendered from the user's uploaded product images)
- The conversion mechanic type (testimonial-led ads stay testimonial-led, headline-led stay headline-led, etc.)

**CHANGES across variations:**
- The visual scene and environment
- The color world and lighting
- The hook mechanic
- The awareness level
- The emotional register
- The copy (headline, subheadline, body, CTA, badge)
- The product positioning style within the same skeleton (hero vs in-use vs hand-held)

This list is the bridge between Phase 1 and Phase 2. Phase 2 uses it to build genuinely distinct variations.
